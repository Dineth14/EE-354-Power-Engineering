Overview

The synchronous machine is the workhorse of electric power generation. Understanding its steady-state phasor diagram, the power-angle relationship, and transient stability via the swing equation is fundamental to power system operation.

Equivalent Circuit

The per-phase equivalent circuit of a round-rotor synchronous machine consists of the internal EMF $E_f$ behind the synchronous impedance:

$$E_f = V_t + I_a (R_a + jX_s)$$

where $V_t$ is the terminal voltage, $I_a$ is the armature current, $R_a$ is the armature resistance (often neglected), and $X_s$ is the synchronous reactance.

Phasor Diagram Relations

Taking $V_t$ as the reference phasor on the real axis and neglecting $R_a$:

1

Generator Mode ($E_f$ leads $V_t$)

The machine delivers power to the grid. The power angle $\delta > 0$.

$$E_f = V_t + jX_s I_a$$

The current lags the terminal voltage by angle $\phi$.

2

Motor Mode ($E_f$ lags $V_t$)

The machine absorbs power from the grid. $\delta < 0$.

$$V_t = E_f + jX_s I_a$$
3

Over- and Under-Excitation

Over-excited ($|E_f| > |V_t|$): supplies reactive power (lagging current). Under-excited ($|E_f| < |V_t|$): absorbs reactive power (leading current).

Power Equations (Round Rotor)

For a round-rotor machine with $R_a = 0$ and $X_s = X_d$:

$$P = \frac{E_f V_t}{X_s} \sin\delta$$
$$Q = \frac{V_t}{X_s}\left(E_f \cos\delta - V_t\right)$$
1

Complex Power Derivation

$$S = V_t I_a^* = V_t \left(\frac{E_f \angle\delta - V_t}{jX_s}\right)^*$$
2

Separate Real and Imaginary Parts

$$I_a = \frac{E_f(\cos\delta + j\sin\delta) - V_t}{jX_s} = \frac{E_f\sin\delta}{X_s} - j\frac{E_f\cos\delta - V_t}{X_s}$$
3

Maximum Power

Maximum power transfer occurs at $\delta = 90°$:

$$P_{max} = \frac{E_f V_t}{X_s}$$

Beyond $\delta = 90°$ the machine loses synchronism (pulls out of step).

Salient-Pole (Two-Reaction) Theory

Salient-pole machines have different reluctance in the d- and q-axes, giving rise to a reluctance torque component.

$$P = \underbrace{\frac{E_f V_t}{X_d}\sin\delta}_{\text{excitation torque}} + \underbrace{\frac{V_t^2}{2}\left(\frac{1}{X_q} - \frac{1}{X_d}\right)\sin 2\delta}_{\text{reluctance torque}}$$
1

Current Decomposition

Armature current decomposed into $d$- and $q$-axis components:

$$I_d = \frac{E_f - V_t\cos\delta}{X_d}, \quad I_q = \frac{V_t\sin\delta}{X_q}$$
2

Voltage Equations

$$V_d = E_f - X_d I_d, \quad V_q = X_q I_q$$
3

Power Expression

$$P = V_d I_d + V_q I_q$$

Substituting and simplifying yields the two-term power equation above. The reluctance torque peak occurs at $\delta = 45°$ and shifts the total power peak below 90°.

Generator Capability Curve

The operating region of a synchronous generator is bounded by:

  • Stator heating limit: $\sqrt{P^2 + Q^2} \leq S_{rated}$ (circle centred at origin)
  • Rotor heating limit: $\sqrt{P^2 + (Q + V_t^2/X_s)^2} \leq E_f V_t / X_s$ (circle centred below Q-axis)
  • Stability limit: $P \leq P_{max}$ (vertical line or $\delta = 90°$ curve)
  • Under-excitation limit: Minimum excitation for practical end-region heating
$$\text{Stator limit: } P^2 + Q^2 = (V_t I_{a,rated})^2$$
$$\text{Rotor limit: } P^2 + \left(Q + \frac{V_t^2}{X_s}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{E_f V_t}{X_s}\right)^2$$

Swing Equation

The equation of motion governing the rotor angle:

$$\frac{2H}{\omega_s}\frac{d^2\delta}{dt^2} = P_m - P_e = P_m - \frac{E_f V_t}{X_s}\sin\delta$$

where $H$ is the inertia constant (MJ/MVA), $\omega_s$ is synchronous speed (rad/s), $P_m$ is mechanical power, and $P_e$ is electrical power.

1

Normalised Form

Defining the per-unit inertia coefficient $M = 2H / \omega_s$:

$$M \ddot{\delta} = P_m - P_e$$
2

Including Damping

$$M \ddot{\delta} + D \dot{\delta} = P_m - P_e$$

where $D$ is the damping coefficient (pu power $\times$ s/rad).

Equal-Area Criterion

A graphical/analytical method for assessing first-swing stability of a single machine connected to an infinite bus.

1

Energy Balance

Multiply the swing equation by $\dot{\delta}$ and integrate:

$$\frac{M}{2}\dot{\delta}^2 = \int_{\delta_0}^{\delta} (P_m - P_e)\, d\delta$$
2

Stability Condition

The machine returns to synchronism if $\dot{\delta}$ returns to zero, i.e., the accelerating area equals the available decelerating area:

$$A_{acc} = \int_{\delta_0}^{\delta_{cl}} (P_m - P_{e,fault})\, d\delta \leq \int_{\delta_{cl}}^{\delta_{max}} (P_{e,post} - P_m)\, d\delta = A_{dec}$$
3

Critical Clearing Angle

The maximum clearing angle $\delta_{cr}$ that maintains stability corresponds to $A_{acc} = A_{dec}$.
