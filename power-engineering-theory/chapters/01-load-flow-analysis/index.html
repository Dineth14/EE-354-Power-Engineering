Load Flow Analysis

The fundamental tool for power system planning, operation, and optimisation

1.1 Introduction

Load flow (or power flow) analysis is the backbone of power system studies. It determines the steady-state operating condition of a power network — the voltage magnitude and angle at every bus, and the real and reactive power flowing through every line. Results are essential for:

  • System planning and expansion studies
  • Economic dispatch and generation scheduling
  • Voltage regulation and reactive power management
  • Contingency analysis (N-1 security)
  • Setting initial conditions for dynamic simulations

1.2 Bus Classification

Every bus in a power system is classified into one of three types based on what quantities are known (specified) versus unknown:

Bus TypeKnownUnknownPurpose
Slack (Swing) Bus$|V|, \delta$ (usually $1.0\angle 0°$)$P, Q$Reference bus; absorbs power mismatch
PV (Generator) Bus$P, |V|$$Q, \delta$Generators with AVR holding voltage
PQ (Load) Bus$P, Q$$|V|, \delta$Loads and unregulated buses
Key insight: A power system with $n$ buses has $2n$ real unknowns ($|V_i|$ and $\delta_i$ for each bus). The slack bus fixes 2 variables; each PV bus specifies 2 ($P, |V|$); each PQ bus specifies 2 ($P, Q$). This yields exactly $2(n-1) - n_{PV}$ independent equations to solve for $n_{PQ}$ magnitude unknowns and $(n-1)$ angle unknowns.

1.3 The Per-Unit System

▶ Derivation: Per-Unit Base Quantities

The per-unit system normalises all quantities to chosen base values, eliminating transformer turns ratios and simplifying calculations.

1
Choose a system-wide base apparent power $S_{base}$ (typically 100 MVA) and a base voltage $V_{base}$ at each voltage level.
2
Base impedance: $$Z_{base} = \frac{V_{base}^2}{S_{base}} = \frac{(kV_{base})^2}{MVA_{base}}$$
3
Base current: $$I_{base} = \frac{S_{base}}{\sqrt{3}\, V_{base}} = \frac{MVA_{base} \times 10^3}{\sqrt{3}\, kV_{base}}$$
4
Per-unit value of any quantity: $$x_{pu} = \frac{x_{actual}}{x_{base}}$$
5
Base conversion — if an impedance is given in old bases $(kV_{old}, MVA_{old})$ and we need it in new bases $(kV_{new}, MVA_{new})$: $$Z_{pu,new} = Z_{pu,old} \times \frac{kV_{old}^2}{kV_{new}^2} \times \frac{MVA_{new}}{MVA_{old}}$$

1.4 Bus Admittance Matrix (Y-Bus)

The bus admittance matrix $\mathbf{Y}_{bus}$ encodes the network topology and impedances in a single $n \times n$ complex matrix. It is the foundation of all power flow calculations.

▶ Formation Algorithm

For each branch connecting bus $i$ to bus $j$ with series impedance $z_{ij} = r_{ij} + jx_{ij}$ and half-line charging susceptance $b_{ij}/2$:

1
Compute the series admittance: $y_{ij} = \frac{1}{z_{ij}} = \frac{r_{ij} - jx_{ij}}{r_{ij}^2 + x_{ij}^2}$
2
Off-diagonal elements (mutual admittance): $$Y_{ij} = Y_{ji} = -y_{ij}$$ The negative of the series admittance between buses $i$ and $j$.
3
Diagonal elements (self admittance): $$Y_{ii} = \sum_{\substack{k=1 \\ k \neq i}}^{n} y_{ik} + \sum_k \frac{b_{ik}}{2}$$ The sum of all admittances connected to bus $i$ (including shunt elements).

Worked Example: 3-Bus Y-Bus

Consider a 3-bus system: $z_{12} = 0.02 + j0.06$ pu, $z_{13} = 0.04 + j0.12$ pu, $z_{23} = 0.03 + j0.09$ pu (no shunt charging).

Series admittances:

$$y_{12} = \frac{1}{0.02+j0.06} = 5.00 - j15.00$$ $$y_{13} = \frac{1}{0.04+j0.12} = 2.50 - j7.50$$ $$y_{23} = \frac{1}{0.03+j0.09} = 3.33 - j10.00$$

Y-bus matrix:

$$\mathbf{Y}_{bus} = \begin{bmatrix} 7.50 - j22.50 & -5.00 + j15.00 & -2.50 + j7.50 \\ -5.00 + j15.00 & 8.33 - j25.00 & -3.33 + j10.00 \\ -2.50 + j7.50 & -3.33 + j10.00 & 5.83 - j17.50 \end{bmatrix}$$

Sparsity: In large systems, most buses connect to only a few neighbours, making $\mathbf{Y}_{bus}$ very sparse — typically 3–5 non-zero entries per row in a 1000-bus system.

1.5 Power Flow Equations

▶ Full Derivation of Power Mismatch Equations

The complex power injected at bus $i$ is:

$$S_i = P_i + jQ_i = V_i I_i^*$$

where the current injection is given by Kirchhoff's current law:

$$I_i = \sum_{k=1}^{n} Y_{ik} V_k$$

Substituting and writing $V_i = |V_i|e^{j\delta_i}$, $Y_{ik} = |Y_{ik}|e^{j\theta_{ik}} = G_{ik} + jB_{ik}$:

$$S_i^* = V_i^* \sum_{k=1}^{n} Y_{ik} V_k$$
1
Expand: $P_i - jQ_i = |V_i|e^{-j\delta_i} \sum_{k=1}^{n} (G_{ik} + jB_{ik})|V_k|e^{j\delta_k}$
2
Separate real and imaginary parts with $\theta_{ik} = \delta_i - \delta_k$:
Active Power $$P_i = \sum_{k=1}^{n} |V_i||V_k|\left(G_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik} + B_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik}\right)$$
Reactive Power $$Q_i = \sum_{k=1}^{n} |V_i||V_k|\left(G_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik} - B_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik}\right)$$

Physical meaning: $G_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik}$ represents the real power transfer component due to the conductance; $B_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik}$ captures the reactive power transfer through the susceptance, driven by the voltage angle difference $\theta_{ik}$. These are $2n$ coupled nonlinear algebraic equations — no closed-form solution exists for systems with more than 2 buses.

1.6 Gauss-Seidel Method

▶ Full Derivation

From $S_i^* = V_i^* \sum_k Y_{ik}V_k$, isolate $V_i$:

1
$$P_i - jQ_i = V_i^* \left(Y_{ii}V_i + \sum_{k \neq i} Y_{ik}V_k\right)$$
2
Rearrange for $V_i$: $$V_i = \frac{1}{Y_{ii}}\left(\frac{P_i - jQ_i}{V_i^*} - \sum_{k \neq i} Y_{ik}V_k\right)$$
3
Iterative update rule (using latest values): $$V_i^{(\nu+1)} = \frac{1}{Y_{ii}}\left(\frac{P_i^{spec} - jQ_i^{spec}}{V_i^{(\nu)*}} - \sum_{k=1}^{i-1} Y_{ik}V_k^{(\nu+1)} - \sum_{k=i+1}^{n} Y_{ik}V_k^{(\nu)}\right)$$
4
Acceleration factor $\omega$: To speed convergence, apply: $$V_i^{acc} = V_i^{(\nu)} + \omega\left(V_i^{(\nu+1)} - V_i^{(\nu)}\right)$$ Typically $1.4 \leq \omega \leq 1.8$. Values outside $[1, 2]$ cause divergence.
Limitations: Gauss-Seidel converges linearly (slow), requires many iterations (50–1000+), and may fail for ill-conditioned networks. It is simple but rarely used for production-grade power flow.

1.7 Newton-Raphson Method

▶ Full Jacobian Matrix Derivation

The Newton-Raphson method solves the nonlinear power flow equations by linearising them at each iteration using the Jacobian matrix.

1
Define the mismatch vector: $$\Delta P_i = P_i^{spec} - P_i^{calc}, \qquad \Delta Q_i = Q_i^{spec} - Q_i^{calc}$$
2
The Jacobian has a $2 \times 2$ block structure: $$\begin{bmatrix} \Delta P \\ \Delta Q \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} J_1 & J_2 \\ J_3 & J_4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta\delta \\ \Delta|V|/|V| \end{bmatrix}$$
3
The four Jacobian sub-matrices (off-diagonal, $i \neq k$): $$J_1: \frac{\partial P_i}{\partial \delta_k} = |V_i||V_k|(G_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik} - B_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik})$$ $$J_2: \frac{\partial P_i}{\partial |V_k|} = |V_i|(G_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik} + B_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik})$$ $$J_3: \frac{\partial Q_i}{\partial \delta_k} = -|V_i||V_k|(G_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik} + B_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik})$$ $$J_4: \frac{\partial Q_i}{\partial |V_k|} = |V_i|(G_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik} - B_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik})$$
4
Diagonal elements ($i = k$) involve sums over all connected buses and include the computed $P_i$, $Q_i$ terms. For example: $$\frac{\partial P_i}{\partial \delta_i} = -Q_i - B_{ii}|V_i|^2$$
5
Update step: $$\begin{bmatrix} \Delta\delta \\ \Delta|V|/|V| \end{bmatrix}^{(\nu)} = \mathbf{J}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta P \\ \Delta Q \end{bmatrix}^{(\nu)}$$ Then: $\delta^{(\nu+1)} = \delta^{(\nu)} + \Delta\delta$, $|V|^{(\nu+1)} = |V|^{(\nu)}(1 + \Delta|V|/|V|)$.
6
Quadratic convergence: The error $\epsilon^{(\nu+1)} \propto C(\epsilon^{(\nu)})^2$. Typically converges in 3–6 iterations regardless of system size. This is because Newton-Raphson incorporates second-order curvature information implicitly through the Jacobian update.
Newton-Raphson Update $$\begin{bmatrix} \Delta\boldsymbol{\delta} \\ \Delta|\mathbf{V}|/|\mathbf{V}| \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} J_1 & J_2 \\ J_3 & J_4 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta\mathbf{P} \\ \Delta\mathbf{Q} \end{bmatrix}$$

1.8 Fast Decoupled Load Flow (FDLF)

▶ $B'$ and $B''$ Matrix Derivation

The FDLF exploits the weak coupling between $P$-$\delta$ and $Q$-$|V|$ in high-voltage transmission systems where $R \ll X$ and angle differences are small.

1
Approximations: $\cos\theta_{ik} \approx 1$, $G_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik} \ll B_{ik}$, and $Q_i \ll B_{ii}|V_i|^2$.
2
This decouples the Jacobian into two independent systems: $$\frac{\Delta P}{|V|} = -B' \cdot \Delta\delta$$ $$\frac{\Delta Q}{|V|} = -B'' \cdot \Delta|V|$$
3
$B'$ is formed from imaginary parts of $Y_{bus}$ neglecting shunt elements and resistance. $B''$ is formed from full imaginary parts of $Y_{bus}$. Both are real, sparse, constant matrices — factored only once!
4
The FDLF iterates: solve for $\Delta\delta$, update angles, compute new $\Delta Q$, solve for $\Delta|V|$, update voltages. Each half-iteration is very cheap.
When FDLF works best: High-voltage transmission networks where $X/R > 4$, angle differences $< 15°$, and no series compensation. It fails for distribution systems or networks with high R/X ratios.

1.9 Voltage Collapse & P-V Curves

▶ Nose Curve Derivation

Consider a simple 2-bus system: sending end at voltage $V_s = 1.0$ pu, load bus receiving $P + jQ$ through a line with reactance $X$.

1
Power equations: $P = \frac{V_s V_r}{X}\sin\delta$, $Q = \frac{V_s V_r \cos\delta - V_r^2}{X}$
2
Eliminating $\delta$ yields a quartic in $V_r^2$: $$V_r^4 + (2XQ - V_s^2)V_r^2 + X^2(P^2 + Q^2) = 0$$
3
The discriminant $\Delta = (2XQ - V_s^2)^2 - 4X^2(P^2+Q^2)$ must be $\geq 0$ for a solution to exist. The boundary $\Delta = 0$ defines the nose point — the maximum loadability.
4
The upper solution (high voltage) is the stable operating point. The lower solution is unstable. At the nose, they merge — voltage collapse. Reactive power compensation shifts the curve upward, increasing the loadability limit.

$$P_i = \sum_k |V_i||V_k|(G_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik} + B_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik})$$

Power Flow (Q)

$$Q_i = \sum_k |V_i||V_k|(G_{ik}\sin\theta_{ik} - B_{ik}\cos\theta_{ik})$$

Gauss-Seidel Update

$$V_i^{(\nu+1)} = \frac{1}{Y_{ii}}\left(\frac{S_i^*}{V_i^{(\nu)*}} - \sum_{k\neq i} Y_{ik}V_k\right)$$

Newton-Raphson

$$\mathbf{x}^{(\nu+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(\nu)} - \mathbf{J}^{-1}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^{(\nu)})$$

Per-Unit Conversion

$$Z_{pu,new} = Z_{pu,old}\frac{kV_{old}^2}{kV_{new}^2}\frac{MVA_{new}}{MVA_{old}}$$

Y-Bus Elements

$$Y_{ii} = \sum_{k\neq i} y_{ik}, \quad Y_{ij} = -y_{ij}$$

$$\Delta P / |V| = -B'\Delta\delta$$$$\Delta Q / |V| = -B''\Delta|V|$$
