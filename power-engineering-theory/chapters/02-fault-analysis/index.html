Overview

Short-circuit faults are the most severe disturbances in power systems. Accurate fault analysis is essential for protective relay coordination, circuit breaker rating, and system stability assessment. In this chapter we develop the mathematical framework for analysing symmetrical (three-phase) and unsymmetrical (SLG, LL, DLG) faults using symmetrical components and the Z-bus method.

Symmetrical (Three-Phase) Faults

A balanced three-phase fault is the most severe but also the simplest to analyse — only the positive-sequence network is involved.

$$I_f^{(3)} = \frac{V_f}{Z_1^{Th} + Z_f}$$

where $V_f$ is the pre-fault voltage at the faulted bus (usually 1.0 p.u.), $Z_1^{Th}$ is the positive-sequence Thevenin impedance, and $Z_f$ is the fault impedance (zero for a bolted fault).

Transient Fault Current Components

A synchronous generator contributes a time-varying fault current with three distinct periods:

1

Subtransient Period (0–3 cycles)

Flux trapped in the damper windings decays with time constant $T''_d$.

$$i''(t) = \left(\frac{1}{X''_d} - \frac{1}{X'_d}\right) e^{-t/T''_d}$$
2

Transient Period (3–30 cycles)

Field winding flux decays with time constant $T'_d$.

$$i'(t) = \left(\frac{1}{X'_d} - \frac{1}{X_d}\right) e^{-t/T'_d}$$
3

Steady-State

Only the synchronous reactance limits the current.

$$I_{ss} = \frac{V_f}{X_d}$$
4

DC Offset (Asymmetrical Component)

The worst-case DC offset decays with time constant $T_a = X''_d / (\omega R_a)$:

$$i_{dc}(t) = \sqrt{2}\, I'' \, e^{-t/T_a}$$

The peak asymmetrical current (first half-cycle) is:

$$I_{asym,peak} = \sqrt{2}\, I'' \left(1 + e^{-\pi / (\omega T_a)}\right)$$

Symmetrical Components (Fortescue's Theorem)

Any set of three unbalanced phasors can be decomposed into three balanced sets: positive-sequence, negative-sequence, and zero-sequence.

$$\begin{bmatrix} V_a \\ V_b \\ V_c \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ a^2 & a & 1 \\ a & a^2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \\ V_0 \end{bmatrix} = [A] \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \\ V_0 \end{bmatrix}$$

The inverse transformation is:

$$\begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \\ V_0 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{3} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & a & a^2 \\ 1 & a^2 & a \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} V_a \\ V_b \\ V_c \end{bmatrix} = [A]^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} V_a \\ V_b \\ V_c \end{bmatrix}$$

where the operator $a = 1\angle 120°= e^{j2\pi/3}$ and $a^2 = 1\angle 240°$, with the property $1 + a + a^2 = 0$.

Worked Example — Decomposing Unbalanced Currents

Given $I_a = 10\angle 0°$, $I_b = 6\angle{-120°}$, $I_c = 8\angle 120°$. Find the sequence components.

Applying $[A]^{-1}$:

$$I_0 = \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + I_b + I_c) = \tfrac{1}{3}(10\angle 0° + 6\angle{-120°} + 8\angle 120°)$$ $$I_1 = \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + aI_b + a^2 I_c)$$ $$I_2 = \tfrac{1}{3}(I_a + a^2 I_b + aI_c)$$

Sequence Networks

Because power system components behave differently for each sequence, we construct three independent networks:

1

Positive-Sequence Network

The normal network with generators modelled by their subtransient/transient reactance behind a voltage source $V_f$. Transformers and lines have their normal impedances.

$$Z_1^{gen} = jX''_d \quad\text{(subtransient studies)}$$
2

Negative-Sequence Network

Same topology as positive-sequence but no voltage sources. Generator negative-sequence reactance:

$$X_2 \approx \frac{X''_d + X''_q}{2}$$

For round-rotor machines $X_2 \approx X''_d$. Transmission line impedances are the same as positive-sequence.

3

Zero-Sequence Network

Depends critically on transformer winding connections and grounding. Zero-sequence current flows only through grounded paths.

$$Z_0^{line} \approx 2\text{–}3.5 \times Z_1^{line}$$

Delta-connected transformer windings block zero-sequence current; grounded-wye connections allow it.

Single Line-to-Ground (SLG) Fault

The most common fault type (~80 % of faults). Fault on phase a to ground through impedance $Z_f$:

Boundary conditions:

  • $I_b = 0,\; I_c = 0$
  • $V_a = Z_f \cdot I_a$
1

Sequence Currents Are Equal

From $I_b = I_c = 0$:

$$I_a^{(1)} = I_a^{(2)} = I_a^{(0)} = \frac{I_a}{3}$$
2

Sequence Network Connection

All three sequence networks connected in series:

$$I_a^{(1)} = \frac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}$$
3

Phase Fault Current

$$I_a = 3I_a^{(1)} = \frac{3V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}$$

Line-to-Line (LL) Fault

Fault between phases b and c through impedance $Z_f$:

Boundary conditions:

  • $I_a = 0$
  • $I_b = -I_c$
  • $V_b - V_c = Z_f \cdot I_b$
1

Sequence Currents

No zero-sequence flows (no ground path):

$$I_a^{(0)} = 0, \quad I_a^{(1)} = -I_a^{(2)}$$
2

Positive and Negative Networks in Parallel

$$I_a^{(1)} = \frac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 + Z_f}$$
3

Phase Fault Current

$$I_b = -I_c = (a^2 - a)\, I_a^{(1)} = -j\sqrt{3}\, I_a^{(1)}$$

When $Z_1 = Z_2$ (common for lines), $\displaystyle |I_b| = \frac{\sqrt{3}\, V_f}{2Z_1 + Z_f}$.

Double Line-to-Ground (DLG) Fault

Phases b and c to ground through $Z_f$:

Boundary conditions:

  • $I_a = 0$
  • $V_b = V_c = Z_f (I_b + I_c)$
1

Positive-Sequence Current

$$I_a^{(1)} = \frac{V_f}{Z_1 + \dfrac{Z_2 (Z_0 + 3Z_f)}{Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}}$$
2

Current Divider for Zero- and Negative-Sequence

$$I_a^{(2)} = -I_a^{(1)} \cdot \frac{Z_0 + 3Z_f}{Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}$$ $$I_a^{(0)} = -I_a^{(1)} \cdot \frac{Z_2}{Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}$$
3

Sequence Network Connection

Negative- and zero-sequence networks connected in parallel, then in series with positive-sequence.

4

Ground Fault Current

$$I_f = I_b + I_c = 3I_a^{(0)}$$

Z-Bus Fault Analysis Method

For large networks, we form the bus impedance matrix $[Z_{bus}]$ for each sequence and extract Thevenin impedances directly:

1

Form $[Y_{bus}]$ for Each Sequence

Build the bus admittance matrix using element impedances for positive, negative, and zero sequence.

$$[Z_{bus}^{(k)}] = [Y_{bus}^{(k)}]^{-1} \quad k \in \{1, 2, 0\}$$
2

Thevenin Impedance at Fault Bus

The diagonal element $Z_{ff}^{(k)}$ is the Thevenin impedance at bus $f$.

$$Z_k^{Th} = Z_{ff}^{(k)}$$
3

Bus Voltages During Fault

For a 3-phase fault at bus $f$:

$$V_i^{(1)} = V_f^{pre} - Z_{if}^{(1)} \cdot I_f^{(1)}$$

This gives voltage depression at every bus — critical for relay coordination.

Quick Reference

3-Phase Fault

$I_f = \dfrac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_f}$

SLG Fault

$I_a^{(1)} = \dfrac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 + Z_0 + 3Z_f}$

LL Fault

$I_a^{(1)} = \dfrac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 + Z_f}$

DLG Fault

$I_a^{(1)} = \dfrac{V_f}{Z_1 + Z_2 \| (Z_0 + 3Z_f)}$

Fortescue Transform

$[A] = \begin{bmatrix}1&1&1\\a^2&a&1\\a&a^2&1\end{bmatrix}$

Operator Identity

$1 + a + a^2 = 0$

Asymmetrical Peak

$I_{pk} = \sqrt{2}\, I''\left(1 + e^{-\pi/\omega T_a}\right)$

Z-Bus Voltage

$V_i = V_f - Z_{if} \cdot I_f$
